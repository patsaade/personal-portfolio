Every alert ends at the same question: **what actually happened on this machine — and can I prove it?** Chasing that question is what pulled me out of IT and into security, and then out of "the alert fired" and into "here's the artifact that proves it." This blog is me going all the way down that path.

## How I got here

I didn't start in forensics. I started on a help desk — imaging machines, resetting passwords, racking switches, and pulling cable across IDFs. It's unglamorous work, but it taught me how enterprise environments actually fit together, which matters more than you'd think when you're trying to reconstruct one after the fact.

From there it was a SOC analyst seat at ReliaQuest, where alerts stopped being noise and started being investigations — and where I moved from triaging detections to tuning and tailoring them to each environment. Then Red Canary, doing hands-on threat response: live response and containment on real endpoints, following EDR telemetry down to what actually executed. Today I work the security operations center at First American.

Every step pulled me one layer closer to the box itself — and forensics is where that road ends up. Not "an EDR flagged this," but *why* it's malicious, *when* it happened, and *how* you'd prove it to someone who wasn't in the room.

## What "going deep" means

Going deep means trading some breadth for the ability to:

- Pull a process out of a memory dump and explain *why* its presence is malicious, not just that something flagged it.
- Reconstruct a timeline from `$MFT`, the USN journal, Prefetch, and registry hives that holds up to scrutiny.
- Follow execution from initial access to persistence to C2, and tie each step to a concrete artifact.

That's the muscle I want to build in public — and the certs I'm working toward (GCFA, GCFE, and GCIH, with the CISSP already done) are pointed in the same direction.

## What you'll find here

I write the posts I wish I'd had when I started, weighted toward the two disciplines I care most about:

1. **Host forensics** — registry persistence, `$MFT`/USN/Prefetch/Shellbags, and timeline reconstruction.
2. **Memory forensics** — Volatility 3, MemProcFS, and finding malicious code in RAM.
3. **EDR artifact analysis** — turning telemetry into a defensible narrative.
4. **Lab & CTF writeups** — CyberDefenders, 13Cubed, MemLabs, Magnet. Full solutions, not just flags.
5. **Tools & exam prep** — the workflow, and notes as I work toward GCFA/GCFE/GCIH.

> The goal isn't to look smart. It's to show the work — the dead ends, the pivots, and the artifact that finally cracked it.

## A note on sources

Everything here comes from **public lab and CTF challenges** — CyberDefenders, 13Cubed, MemLabs, Magnet — and my own home lab. **Nothing from any employer's casework appears on this site**: no client data, no real-incident details. Public datasets are the point; they let me show the full work in the open, end to end.

If you're on the same road — toward GCFA, toward a forensics-heavy role, toward actually understanding the box instead of just the alert — pull up a chair. Let's get into it.
